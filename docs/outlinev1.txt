Building your own matching engine forces you to understand market microstructure—tick rules, margin states, and exactly how your own orders would theoretically interact with a live order book. Here is the blueprint for your localized testing environment. We are breaking it down into three core modules:

### 1. The Data Ingestion Engine (The Eyes)

You need public market data. Hyperliquid’s public endpoints and WebSockets, such as requests for order books or candles, are read-only and do not require you to include a wallet address in the request body.

* **The Action:** Connect to the `l2Book` (Level 2 Order Book) WebSocket stream.
* **The Goal:** Maintain a localized, real-time mirror of the Hyperliquid order book in your machine's memory.

### 2. The Virtual State Manager (The Ledger)

This is your localized exchange database.

* **The Variables:** Initialize a mock balance (e.g., `1000 USDC`). Track your `open_positions`, `margin_used`, and `pnl`.
* **The Rule:** Every simulated trade must deduct virtual fees (maker/taker) and update the ledger. If your virtual margin drops below maintenance, write a function that liquidates the position. Do not coddle the code.

### 3. The Matching Engine (The Executioner)

When your bot logic decides to place a trade, it routes here instead of to CCXT.

* **Market Orders:** Check the real-time L2 book and "execute" against the available volume at the best bid or ask.
* **Limit Orders:** Log the order locally. Your engine must monitor the incoming WebSocket trades; if the real market price crosses your simulated limit price, trigger a simulated fill.
* **The "Slippage" Penalty:** Do not assume perfect fills. If you simulate a market order, build in a randomized latency delay (e.g., 50–150ms) and calculate the fill against where the book *moved* during that delay, not where it was when you hit send.

### The Hyperliquid Nuance

To make the simulation actually valuable, you must strictly enforce Hyperliquid's exchange rules locally:

* **Tick & Lot Sizes:** Orders are only added where the price is an exact integer multiple of the tick size, and the size is an integer multiple of the lot size.
* **Price Precision:** Prices have a maximum of 5 significant figures. If your bot submits a price like `64532.12`, the real exchange will reject it. Your simulator must reject it, too.
